First steps

We started Arctic Monkeys in 2002 as a group of friends from Sheffield, playing in garages and doing a few local gigs. It was just Alex (vocals, guitar), Jamie (guitar), Andy (bass), and Matt (drums), having fun making music. Things really took off people began sharing our recordings online, especially on MySpace.

2004 Sheffield Rock Festival
2005 Manchester Festival
2006 - Melbrook Rock Bar
2007 - London Boulevard

By the time we released “I Bet You Look Good on the Dancefloor” in 2005, it went straight to number one in the UK. Our debut album, "Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not" (2006), became the fastest-selling debut in UK history. It all happened naturally—through gigs and word-of-mouth, without a big campaign, just music that connected with people.

Our first album

Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not

We weren’t thinking about making a classic or changing music; we were just documenting the nights out , the streets we walked, and the characters we met. It’s fast, loud, and raw, just like the gigs we were playing in those early days. A lot of the songs were written about the chaos of being out late , getting into scrapes, and all the stuff that seemed important back then. It’s mad to think it resonated with so many people, but it was honest, and I guess people heard that. We had no idea it’d take off like it did. It’s all there, a snapshot of who we were at the time, before all the madness kicked off.

Our History and styles

Favourite Worst Nightmare and Our Sonic Growth

After the whirlwind success of our debut album, we released Favourite Worst Nightmare in 2007. It was faster, heavier, and showcased how much we had evolved in such a short time. Tracks like "Brianstorm" and "Fluorescent Adolescent" became staples of our live sets, packed with the energy that had always defined us.

A Darker Shift: Humbug

By 2009, we were ready to shake things up. With Humbug, produced by Josh Homme, we leaned into a heavier, more psychedelic sound. Songs like "Crying Lightning" reflected this shift, and while it caught some fans off guard, it marked an important moment for us: the realization that we never wanted to stay in one place musically.

Till today

By 2011, "Suck It and See" took us back to a lighter, more melodic sound , but it was our fifth album, "AM" (2013), that really brought us global recognition . Songs like "Do I Wanna Know?" and "R U Mine?" helped us break into the U.S. market, earning critical acclaim and commercial success worldwide.

2011 Sentrum Scene - Oslo
2018 - Cavea-Rome

We took a bit of a break after that, and it wasn’t until 2018 that we dropped our next album, "Tranquility Base Hotel & Casino". It was a big shift for us—a lot more experimental and slower, with a spacey, lounge vibe. Some people were surprised by the change in direction, but we wanted to try something different . It wasn’t what everyone expected, but we were proud of it and felt it was important to keep evolving.

Last Album

Arctic monkeys
the car

With The Car, we wanted to push our sound even further . It’s got strings, orchestration, and a cinematic vibe, something we’ve been leaning into more since Tranquility Base. Lyrically, Alex reflects on nostalgia, fame, and the passage of time . It’s not trying to be what people might expect from us, but more about following our instincts—just like we did back in the Sheffield garage. Songs like "There’d Better Be a Mirrorball" and "Body Paint" capture that mood, blending lush production with some of our most introspective work to date .
